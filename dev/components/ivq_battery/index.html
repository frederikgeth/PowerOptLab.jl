Current–voltage (IVQ) battery

Kind: Component model · Maturity: prototype · Direction: forward · Temporal: single-snapshot + multi-period

IVQBattery models grid storage in the voltage–current–charge variable space instead of the power–energy space of StorageDevice. It is the Julia counterpart to the model of

P. Aaslid, F. Geth, M. Korpås, M. M. Belsnes, O. B. Fosso, Non-linear charge-based battery storage optimization model with bi-variate cubic spline constraints, Journal of Energy Storage 32 (2020) 101979. doi:10.1016/j.est.2020.101979

Where the power–energy ("PE") model — StorageDevice plus the state-of-charge balance in solve_multiperiod_opf — tracks energy with a fixed round-trip efficiency, the IVQ model tracks charge and represents the cell terminal voltage explicitly, so voltage and current limits are enforced individually rather than folded into a conservative power limit. Two consequences matter for dispatch:

  • Safe operation up to the cell boundaries. A PE-model must pad its power and SoC limits to guarantee the true voltage/current limits are never crossed; the IVQ model carries those limits directly and can use the full envelope.
  • A current-dependent efficiency proxy is derived, not assumed. $\eta = f_v(\text{soc}, i)\,/\,f_v(\text{soc}, -i)$ falls out of the voltage curve and worsens with current — no eff_charge/eff_discharge parameters. Note this is an instantaneous, equal-current, fixed-SoC cell efficiency, not a full round-trip energy efficiency over a cycle, and it excludes the converter losses (which live on the AdvancedInverter).

Terminal-voltage model

The paper represents the cell voltage as a bivariate cubic spline $v = f_v(\text{soc}, i)$ fitted to multi-C-rate discharge data. That surface needs data published for almost no chemistry, so PowerOptLab instead uses the 0th-order equivalent-circuit (Thévenin / Rint) decomposition

\[v(\text{soc}, i) = \mathrm{OCV}(\text{soc}) - i \cdot R(\text{soc}), \qquad i > 0 \ \text{discharge},\]

which captures the same qualitative behaviour — terminal voltage sags under discharge and rises under charge — from an open-circuit-voltage curve (abundant and citable for every chemistry) plus an internal resistance (from HPPC pulse data). It is not the same physics as the empirical surface: Rint omits polarization dynamics, relaxation, rate dependence and hysteresis (which matter especially for LFP — a hysteresis-aware RC model is the usual next step). The pack scales the cell by its series/parallel counts: $v_\text{pack} = n_\text{series}\,v_\text{cell}$, $i_\text{pack} = n_\text{parallel}\,i_\text{cell}$, and the DC power is $p_\text{dc} = v_\text{cell}\,i_\text{cell}\,n_\text{series}\,n_\text{parallel}$.

Three fidelity levels, cheapest first — each a BatteryChemistry:

The PE-model is the further special case of thevenin_chemistry with R = 0 (constant voltage ⇒ power ∝ current).

OCV(soc) smoothness — and its limits

In multi-period, OCV(soc) is evaluated at the SoC variable. linear/thevenin OCV is affine (C^∞, embedded directly). tabulated OCV uses a monotone cubic (PCHIP) interpolant, which is C¹ but not C² at interior knots and only C⁰ at the two outer knots (where it clamps flat to forbid extrapolation). Ipopt's theory assumes twice-continuously-differentiable functions, so PCHIP is a pragmatic choice, not a guaranteed-convergent one — in practice it works because evaluations rarely land exactly on a knot, and the soc_min/soc_max window is required to sit strictly inside the outer knots so the C⁰ points are never in range. A monotone C² alternative (shape-constrained smoothing) is the robust upgrade. PCHIP is preferred over a plain cubic spline (C² but overshoots, handing an optimiser free voltage) because monotonicity matters more than the extra derivative here.

Reusing the converter

The AC-side converter is not re-implemented. An AdvancedInverter owns the point-of-connection coupling, output filter, converter losses, and apparent-power / three-phase topology limits; the battery attaches to its DC port. In this first version the coupling is at the DC power level,

\[v_\text{cell}\,i_\text{cell}\,n_\text{series}\,n_\text{parallel} \;=\; P_\text{dc}^{\text{(inverter)}} \quad [\text{W}],\]

which is exact for the default single-phase inverter (whose v_dc only enters an optional modulation cap). Making v_dc a shared decision variable equal to the battery terminal voltage — so the three-phase switching-polytope sees the true, SoC-dependent DC rail — is the natural next step; until then set the inverter's v_dc to the nominal pack voltage.

Usage

using PowerOptLab

chem = illustrative_lfp()                       # or nmc/nca/lead_acid/leaf, or build your own
inv  = AdvancedInverter(id="bat", bus="poc", s_max=5000.0)
bat  = IVQBattery(id="bat", bus="poc", chemistry=chem,
                  n_series=100, n_parallel=1, soc_init=0.6, inverter=inv)

r = solve_ivq_battery(net, bat; objective=:max_export)
r.p_poc      # power delivered to the grid (W)
r.v_cell     # cell terminal voltage under load (V) — below OCV when discharging
r.i_cell     # signed cell current (A, > 0 discharge)
r.p_dc       # DC-link power = p_conv + p_loss

objective is :max_export (discharge to a cell voltage/current or converter limit), :max_charge, or :min_loss (with a p_set delivery target). SoC is fixed at soc_init — this single operating point reproduces the paper's second case study: the deliverable power set by the cell's own voltage and current limits, which a power-only model misses.

Multi-period arbitrage

solve_multiperiod_ivq co-optimises a chronological sequence of snapshots with the SoC linking the periods — now soc is a decision variable, so OCV(soc) enters the model as a function of it (embedded for affine chemistries; a registered smooth operator for tabulated ones).

nets = [net_expensive, net_cheap]            # e.g. differing voltage_source `cost`
bat  = IVQBattery(id="bat", bus="poc", chemistry=illustrative_lfp(),
                  n_series=300, n_parallel=1, soc_init=0.5, inverter=inv, cyclic=true)
res  = solve_multiperiod_ivq(nets, [bat]; dt_h=1.0)
res.dispatch["bat"].soc      # SoC trajectory (length T+1)
res.dispatch["bat"].i_cell   # signed cell current per period

The charge balance is a forward update $q_{t+1} = q_t - i_t\,\Delta t$, which is exact for the piecewise-constant current of each period (there is one current variable per period), so it conserves charge exactly — for a cyclic horizon the closure $\sum_t i_t\,\Delta t = 0$ is enforced term-for-term. (A true trapezoidal rule would need currents at all $T{+}1$ time nodes with consistent endpoint semantics; the earlier per-interval average silently broke coulomb conservation and was removed.) Terminal state is set by cyclic=true (return to soc_init) or soc_final. The objective is the horizon energy cost (each snapshot's cost rate $\times\,\Delta t$).

Nonconvex — expect local optima

The coupled cell + inverter model over several periods is nonconvex (p = OCV(soc)·i is bilinear on top of the rectangular power flow), so solve_multiperiod_ivq finds a local optimum and does not converge for every configuration. A non-LOCALLY_SOLVED/OPTIMAL status returns NaN trajectories rather than an unconverged point — always check the status. A PE-model warm start (solve solve_multiperiod_opf first, seed the dispatch) is the planned robustness improvement.

Scaling: per-unit and SI

The battery is modelled in pack quantities, per-unit on its own DC bases (pack nominal voltage and max discharge current), so the current and voltage variables are ≈ O(1) independent of the engine's s_base. This is what keeps the coupled solve conditioned — a naïve SI formulation mixes cell volts (~3.5 V), network kV, and kW in one problem, spanning five orders of magnitude, and the solver then returns poor points. The DC↔AC coupling carries the s_base factor explicitly, so solve_ivq_battery and solve_multiperiod_ivq work in both the engine's per-unit (per_unit=true, the default) and raw-SI (per_unit=false) modes; results are returned in SI either way. Per-unit additionally conditions the AC-network side of the coupled solve, and the nonconvex multi-period problem converges far more reliably under it across platforms/Ipopt builds — a raw-SI solve of the same problem is knife-edge (small solver-option changes flip it between LOCALLY_SOLVED and LOCALLY_INFEASIBLE), which is why per-unit is the default.

Chemistry presets and data sources

The presets are illustrative, not measured

The illustrative_* presets — illustrative_lfp, illustrative_nmc, illustrative_nca, illustrative_lead_acid, illustrative_leaf — are hand-drawn OCV shapes in a plausible voltage band for each chemistry. They are not fits to any cell's measured data; they carry no temperature, state-of-health, or rest-protocol metadata; and their capacity / current / resistance defaults are representative round numbers, not tied to the cell that inspired the voltage band. Use them for demos, tests and defaults — not for scientific comparison or operational studies. For a calibrated chemistry, fit real data (below) and pass it through tabulated_chemistry.

LFP is provided first because it is common in residential/stationary storage and its flat plateau with sharp end-knees is a good stress case — the PE-model's constant-voltage assumption is least wrong mid-SoC yet the voltage/current limits bite hardest at the knees.

A proper chemistry library — named, versioned cell parameter sets with stored raw/licensed data, deterministic fitting scripts, separate provenance for OCV / resistance / capacity / limits, and voltage-error validation over SoC, current, temperature and charge/discharge direction — is future work, not what these presets provide.

Critique of the source model (and how this differs)

Honest notes on the 2020 paper, and the choices made here:

  • Data availability. The bivariate f_v(soc, i) surface needs multi-C-rate data at matched SoC — a dead-end for a chemistry library. The OCV + R decomposition recovers the same physics from data that actually exists, turning "one chemistry" into "any chemistry with a published OCV curve".
  • Optimiser vs. simulation. The paper's spline smoothing factor is chosen visually, and cubic extrapolation can produce non-physical voltages (its Fig. 2). Harmless when simulating; a correctness bug when an optimiser can park the state where the fit hands out free energy. Hence the monotone, clamped OCV here.
  • Converter assumptions. The paper assumes a stable AC voltage and DC-side- only converter losses — which break in an unbalanced four-wire feeder, exactly where BMOPFTools operates. Reusing AdvancedInverter, with its explicit internal AC node and current-dependent losses, repairs that.
  • Non-convexity. p = v·i with v = f(soc) is bilinear-nonconvex on top of the already-nonconvex rectangular power flow; only local optima. The PE-model (StorageDevice) is the natural convex warm-start — a planned option for the multi-period solve.
  • Excluded. Temperature and degradation (both affect OCV and especially R) are out of scope for v1; R(soc) leaves room to add a T dependence later.

Status and next steps

Delivered: the chemistry library, the single-snapshot device (solve_ivq_battery), and multi-period arbitrage (solve_multiperiod_ivq) with the exact forward charge balance and per-unit conditioning. Planned:

  1. PE warm-start for the multi-period solve — seed from solve_multiperiod_opf to put Ipopt in the right basin on the harder (e.g. net-charge-against-price) configurations that currently fail to converge.
  2. Shared v_dc: couple the inverter DC rail to the battery terminal voltage (so the three-phase switching-polytope sees the true SoC-dependent DC rail).
  3. Temperature / degradation hooks on R(soc) and capacity.
  4. Co-optimise IVQ batteries alongside PE StorageDevices in one horizon.
